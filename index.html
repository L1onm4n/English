Chapter 1

The Long March
to Freedom

After Reconstruction, Black Americans fought to overthrow a system of subjugation, persecution and suffering — with dignity and courage.

1921
The Tulsa Massacre — "Black Wall Street" burned to the ground A White mob destroyed the most affluent Black community in the US. 35 blocks razed. Hundreds killed. 10,000 left homeless. Nobody was ever prosecuted.
  • Freed families were promised 40 acres and a mule — President Johnson returned the land to Confederates instead
  • Many were forced into sharecropping — working land for a slice of the harvest, trapped in perpetual debt
  • Scores fled north to Chicago, Detroit, New York, LA — found racism and hostility there too
  • The 13th, 14th & 15th Amendments were rapidly undermined by the Supreme Court (1883)
  • Total segregation from birth to death — Black hospitals, Black graveyards, "Whites only" signs everywhere
  • "Grandfather Clause" — can only vote if your grandfather could. Descendants of enslaved people: excluded
  • Debt peonage — wages manipulated to keep Black workers in a permanent debt cycle, working for free
  • 3,446 lynchings by the KKK and white supremacist groups between 1882 and 1968
  • In 1921, Tulsa's "Black Wall Street" — the most affluent Black community in the US — burned to the ground. 10,000 left homeless. Nobody prosecuted.
  • NAACP founded 1909 — USA's oldest and largest civil rights organisation
  • First president Moorfield Storey argued before the Supreme Court that Louisville had violated the 14th Amendment
  • The Harlem Renaissance (1920s) — a cultural explosion of Black art, literature, music and intellectual life in New York
  • W.E.B. Du Bois called it a "spiritual coming of age" transforming social disillusionment into pride
  • Emmett Till (1955) — 14-year-old tortured and murdered in Mississippi. Open casket. All-White jury acquitted the killers. Galvanised the movement.
  • Rosa Parks (Dec 1955) — arrest sparked a 381-day bus boycott led by Dr. MLK → buses desegregated
  • March on Washington (1963) — 260,000 gathered. MLK delivered "I Have a Dream." Photos of children attacked by police dogs shocked the world.
  • Civil Rights Act 1964 — bans discrimination by race, colour, religion, sex or national origin. Passed after an 83-day Senate filibuster.
  • Voting Rights Act 1965 — bans literacy tests. Black voter registration rose from 35% to 65%.
  • Selma to Montgomery (1965) — 500 marchers attacked by troopers on national TV. March grew to 25,000.

"Jim Crow laws formed the pillars of a socioeconomic system designed to keep Black people subjugated in poverty."

Chapter 2

The Land of
the Free...?

The 13th Amendment abolished slavery — but included a loophole that has allowed the penal system to repress minority communities ever since.

"Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime … shall exist within the United States."

— 13th Amendment, 1865

357K→2.3M
The prison population exploded under the War on Drugs From 357,000 in 1981 to 2.3 million by 2000 — driven by Nixon's coded "dog whistle" policies and Clinton's 1994 Crime Bill, which introduced mandatory life sentences after two prior convictions.
  • After abolition, Southern lawmakers immediately criminalised the emancipated Black population — arrested for petty crimes like vagrancy, set straight back to work
  • D.W. Griffith's 1915 film Birth of a Nation demonised Black men and was almost solely responsible for the rebirth of the KKK — membership soon reached 3 million
  • The film is still in use by sections of the white supremacy movement today
  • Convicts were leased to agricultural businesses in the South — a continuation of slavery by another name
  • Nixon declared drugs "Public Enemy No. 1" — using coded "dog whistle" language to target Black communities without naming them
  • 1 oz of crack = same sentence as 100 oz of cocaine — wealthier, Whiter cocaine users not targeted to the same degree
  • Prison population: 357,000 (1981) → 513,000 (1989) → 760,000 (1995) → 2.3 million (2000)
  • Clinton's 1994 Crime Bill — "three strikes" provision: mandatory life sentence after two prior convictions, including drug crimes
  • The Bill also militarised small local police forces with SWAT provisions
  • Private companies build and run prisons — they need full prisons to protect their investment
  • ALEC (American Legislative Exchange Council) — politicians + corporations (Walmart, Coca-Cola) — allows businesses to directly shape legislation
  • CCA (now CoreCivic): $11M per month from illegal immigrants detained in Arizona alone
  • Convicted felons often lose the right to vote — even after release — a direct echo of post-slavery disenfranchisement
  • Free prison labour: companies including Boeing, Microsoft, JC Penney, Victoria's Secret have used prisoners manufacturing products for pennies

Chapter 3

Legacy &
Lasting Impact

400+ years of trafficking left consequences in every corner of the world — in healthcare, policing, wealth, governance and culture.

Systemic Racism
  • Black Americans: 3.57× more likely to die from Covid-19
  • Many White medical students still hold false beliefs about Black pain tolerance — identical to 19th-century justifications for enslaved people's mistreatment
  • UK: over ⅓ of BME workers unfairly turned down for jobs; 15% singled out for redundancy due to racism (TUC 2021)
  • African & Bangladeshi households hold 10× less wealth than White households in the UK
  • 55% of Black & mixed-race Covid patients in Brazil died, vs 38% of White patients
Policing — Origins & Today
  • 1661: Barbados passed the first "slave law" — direct precursor to modern police
  • London Metropolitan Police (1829) inspired by Patrick Colquhoun — who owned shares in Jamaican sugar plantations
  • US first police forces: created to control enslaved people, not protect all citizens
  • Black Americans: ~3× more likely to be killed by police than White Americans
  • UK: Black people 8× more likely to be stopped & searched; 43× more likely under Section 60
Reparations & Money
  • UK took out £20M loan in 1833 to compensate 46,000 slave owners — not paid off until 2015. Enslaved people: nothing.
  • France extorted reparations from Haiti for 100+ years — for "loss of property" when the enslaved people revolted
  • UCL 2012: 10–20% of Britain's wealthiest have significant links to slavery
  • Confederate slave owners received compensation after the Civil War — the enslaved did not
  • The Black-White wealth gap in the US traces directly to centuries of systematic wealth extraction
Africa & Modern Slavery
  • 20 million guns imported to Africa in the second half of the 18th century — many made in Birmingham, UK
  • Trafficking destroyed governance systems based on kinship, consent and trust — creating long-term instability
  • 40.3 million people in modern slavery today — 24.9M in forced labour, 15.4M in forced marriage
  • Women and girls = 71% of modern slavery victims
  • Most prevalent in Africa, then Asia and the Pacific — a largely invisible global crisis

"Black people in the US were 3.57 times more likely to die from Covid-19 — the disparity in these figures is no surprise when we look at the documented history of structural inequalities."

Chapter 4

The Fight
Continues

From BLM to reparations, statue removal to language reform — the struggle for justice is very much alive today.

26M
George Floyd's murder (2020) triggered one of the largest protest movements in US history Between 15 and 26 million people took to the streets across the United States alone. BLM protests spread globally — Britain, Brazil, Colombia, Denmark, France, Canada and beyond.
  • Founded 2013 by Alicia Garza, Patrisse Cullors & Opal Tometi — after acquittal of George Zimmerman for murder of Trayvon Martin
  • After George Floyd's murder (2020): 15–26 million protesters in the US alone — one of the largest movements in US history
  • Active globally: Britain, Brazil, Colombia, Denmark, France, Canada and beyond
  • 2021 UN Human Rights Council resolution: investigate root causes of systemic racism and police violence worldwide
  • BLM has grown into a decentralised political and social movement fighting legacies of slavery, police brutality and racism
  • Over 170 Confederate monuments removed in the US between June 2020 and June 2021
  • Germany (2021) — announced return of hundreds of artefacts to Benin, West Africa
  • Church of England — publicly apologised for inheriting three Caribbean sugar estates run with forced labour
  • Multiple UK banks & institutions issued public apologies for slave trade ties since 2020
  • Critics: "An apology as a public spectacle is not enough. It is not a PR exercise."
  • Goal: redirect funding to mental health, homelessness and rehabilitation — not abolish police entirely
  • 25% of people shot by police in a 6-month period were experiencing a mental health crisis (Washington Post, 2015)
  • Sweden (since 2015) — mental health professionals deployed instead of police officers for relevant calls
  • 2020: over a dozen US cities cut police budgets by $1.4 billion+
