The Scale of
the Crime

We can't be sure how many African people were enslaved — most records count only those who arrived alive, not the millions who died during raids, transport within Africa, or the Middle Passage.

400+ Years of transatlantic trafficking
30M Africans forcibly uprooted (UNESCO)
40.3M People in modern slavery today
3.57× More likely — Black Americans to die from Covid-19

  • The trauma, death and oppression over such an extended period left a deep, lasting impact on both the captives and the captors — from West Africa to Britain, France, Brazil, the US, the Caribbean and South America.

  • Records only count people who arrived alive. Millions more died during raids in Africa, transport across the continent, or during the horrifying Middle Passage across the Atlantic.

  • The lasting legacies are woven intricately into the fabric of our societies — in healthcare, policing, housing, education, employment, and culture.

Chapter 1

The Long March
to Freedom

After Reconstruction, Black Americans gave their lives to overthrow a system of subjugation, persecution and suffering — with dignity and courage.

After the Civil War

  • Freed families were promised 40 acres and a mule — but President Andrew Jackson returned the land to former Confederates.

  • Many were forced into sharecropping: working land for a portion of the harvest, trapped in debt peonage and exploitation.

  • Scores migrated north and west to Chicago, Detroit, New York and Los Angeles — only to encounter racism and hostility there, too.

  • The 13th, 14th & 15th Amendments were rapidly undermined. In 1883, the Supreme Court ruled states couldn't discriminate — but a patchwork of local laws did.

Jim Crow Laws

  • Jim Crow laws formalised total segregation — from birth (Black hospitals) to death (Black graveyards). "Whites only" signs over bus stops, fountains, toilets and entrances.

  • The "Grandfather Clause" barred anyone from voting whose grandfather hadn't voted — effectively disenfranchising all descendants of enslaved people.

  • Many White employers practised debt peonage — manipulating wages to trap Black workers in a permanent cycle of debt, working for free.

  • Between 1882 and 1968, 3,446 Black people were lynched by the KKK and other white supremacist hate groups.

"Jim Crow laws formed the pillars of a socioeconomic system designed to keep Black people subjugated in poverty."

Chapter 1 — Key Events

The Fight
for Civil Rights

1921
Massacre on Black Wall Street
Tulsa's Greenwood District — the most affluent Black community in the US — was burned to the ground by a White mob. 35 city blocks razed, 800+ injured, hundreds killed, 10,000 left homeless. No one was ever prosecuted.
1920s
Harlem Renaissance
New York's Harlem neighbourhood became a beacon of Black culture, art, literature, music and intellectual life — giving Black people a powerful platform to express the African-American experience.
Aug 1955
Emmett Till
14-year-old Emmett Till was tortured and murdered in Mississippi. His mother insisted on an open casket. The all-White jury acquitted the killers — who later confessed to Look magazine. His funeral galvanised the Civil Rights Movement.
Dec 1955
Rosa Parks & the Bus Boycott
Rosa Parks' arrest sparked a 381-day citywide boycott spearheaded by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., resulting in the desegregation of Montgomery's buses.
1957
Little Rock, Arkansas
President Eisenhower was forced to send federal troops to escort nine Black students into a previously all-White high school after the governor used the National Guard to block them.
1963
March on Washington
260,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial for the March on Jobs and Freedom. Dr. King delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech. Photos of children being clubbed and bitten by police dogs shocked the nation.
1964
Civil Rights Act
After an 83-day Senate filibuster, the Civil Rights Act banned discrimination by race, colour, religion, sex or national origin. The Voting Rights Act followed in 1965 — Black voter registration rose from 35% to 65%.
1965
Selma to Montgomery
Dr. King led 500 marchers 54 miles from Selma to Montgomery. They were attacked by state troopers on national TV. The march grew to 25,000 and directly prompted the Voting Rights Act.

Chapter 2

The Land of
the Free...?

The 13th Amendment abolished slavery — but included a loophole that has allowed the American penal system to repress minority classes ever since.

"Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States."

— 13th Amendment, US Constitution, 1865

  • One in every 17 White males is expected to spend time in prison. For Black males: one in three.

  • Black males are 6.5% of the US population but make up over 40% of prison inmates.

  • After abolition, Southern lawmakers immediately criminalised the emancipated Black population — arresting Black men for petty crimes like vagrancy and loitering, then setting them to work under the "punishment for crime" clause.

  • D.W. Griffith's 1915 film Birth of a Nation — which demonised Black men — was almost singularly responsible for the rebirth of the Ku Klux Klan. Its iconography is still in use by the white supremacy movement today.

The War on Drugs

  • President Nixon introduced "dog whistle politics" — coded, racialised language — to garner Southern support without explicitly naming targets. He declared drugs "Public Enemy No. 1."

  • The sentence for 1 oz of crack was the same as for 100 oz of cocaine — disproportionately targeting poorer Black communities over Whiter, wealthier cocaine users.

  • From 1981 to 1989, the prison population grew from 357,000 to 513,000. By 2000, it stood at over 2.3 million.

  • Clinton's 1994 Crime Bill introduced the "three strikes" provision — mandatory life sentences after two prior convictions, including drug crimes — and massively militarised local police forces.

The Prison-Industrial Complex (PIC)

  • Privately owned companies build and run prisons — and must ensure their investment is protected. They require full prisons.

  • The American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) — comprised of politicians and corporations including Walmart and Coca-Cola — allows companies to introduce legislative ideas voted on by politicians.

  • CCA (now CoreCivic) describes itself as "the nation's leading provider of high-quality corrections and detention management."

  • The 13th Amendment granted freedom to the enslaved — but has also attacked those freedoms across a complicated and often cruel history that is still evolving today.

Chapter 3

Legacy &
Lasting Impact

More than 400 years of transatlantic trafficking left consequences that reach into every corner of the world — and into every aspect of daily life today.

Systemic Racism

  • Black people in the US were 3.57× more likely to die from Covid-19 than White people (2020 study).

  • A 2016 study found many White medical students still held false beliefs about Black pain tolerance — the same beliefs used to justify inhumane treatment of the enslaved.

  • A 2021 TUC survey found over a third of BME workers in Britain had been unfairly turned down for a job; 15% reported being singled out for redundancy due to racist treatment.

  • African and Bangladeshi households hold ten times less wealth than White households in the UK (Runnymede, 2020).

Policing — Origins & Today

  • In 1661, Barbados passed its first "slave law." Slave codes spread to English and European colonies worldwide. These were the direct precursors to modern policing.

  • Patrick Colquhoun — who owned shares in Jamaican sugar plantations — inspired Robert Peel to establish the London Metropolitan Police in 1829.

  • In the US, the first police forces were created to control enslaved people and protect White communities — not to serve all citizens.

  • African Americans are ~3× more likely to be killed by police than White Americans. In the UK, Black people are 8× more likely to be stopped and searched.

Reparations & Wealth

  • In 2016, UK taxpayers found out they'd only just finished repaying the £20M loan Britain took out in 1833 to compensate 46,000 slave owners — not the enslaved.

  • France extorted reparations from Haiti for over a century — paid to enslavers, not to the formerly enslaved — for "loss of property" after the Haitian Revolution.

  • UCL's 2012 project found 10–20% of Britain's wealthiest people have significant links to slavery.

  • Since 2020 BLM protests, several UK financial institutions issued public apologies for ties to the slave trade — but many argue apologies are not enough.

Impact on Africa & Modern Slavery

  • An estimated 20 million guns were imported to Africa in the second half of the 18th century — many manufactured in Birmingham, UK — destabilising entire regions.

  • The trafficking destroyed existing governance systems based on kinship, consent and trust, creating long-term economic and political instability.

  • The Global Slavery Index (2016) found 40.3 million people in modern slavery — 24.9M in forced labour, 15.4M in forced marriage. Women & girls = 71% of victims.

  • Modern slavery is most prevalent in Africa, followed by Asia and the Pacific. It remains a largely invisible global crisis.

Chapter 4

The Fight
Continues

From Black Lives Matter to reparations, statue removal to language reform — the struggle for justice is very much alive.

Black Lives Matter

  • Founded in 2013 by Alicia Garza, Patrisse Cullors & Opal Tometi after the acquittal of George Zimmerman for the murder of Trayvon Martin.

  • After the murder of George Floyd by Minneapolis officer Derek Chauvin in 2020, an estimated 15–26 million people participated in protests in the US — one of the largest movements in American history.

  • BLM has grown into a decentralised global movement — active in Britain, Brazil, Colombia, Denmark, France, Canada and beyond.

  • In 2021, the UN Human Rights Council adopted a resolution to investigate the root causes of systemic racism and police violence worldwide.

Monuments & Memory

  • Over 170 Confederate monuments were removed across the US between June 2020 and June 2021.

  • In 2021, Germany announced it would return hundreds of stolen artefacts to Benin in West Africa, pillaged during the colonial era.

  • The Church of England publicly apologised for inheriting three Caribbean sugar estates run with the forced labour of enslaved people.

  • Many anti-racism campaigners say: "It is not enough to make your apology as a public spectacle — it is not enough to present it as a PR exercise."

Language Matters

  • Say "enslaved people" or "captives" — not "slaves"

  • Say "trafficking of enslaved Africans" — not "slave trade"

  • Use "enslaver" — not "master" or "owner"

  • Say "self-liberated" or "freedom fighter" — not "runaway slave"

  • Consider "stolen labour, knowledge and skills" — not just "stolen labour"

Defunding the Police?

  • Advocates say the goal is simply to invest in services that address community issues — mental health, homelessness, rehabilitation — rather than policing.

  • In a 2015 Washington Post analysis, 25% of those shot and killed by police in a 6-month period were experiencing a mental health crisis.

  • Sweden has since 2015 deployed mental health professionals instead of police officers for relevant calls — freeing up police for serious crime.

  • In 2020, over a dozen US city governments moved to reduce police budgets by more than $1.4 billion.

"The wounds of slavery are still open and festering."

The lasting impacts of the transatlantic slave trade are so huge, widespread and complex that they cannot be fully covered here. We all have a responsibility to educate ourselves on these legacies — and to consider what actions we can take to help create a more equal society that is not modelled on such a horrifying history.
