01 Chapter One

The Long March
to Freedom

After Reconstruction, Black Americans fought to overthrow a system of subjugation, persecution and suffering — with dignity and courage. But every advance was met with new forms of oppression.

After the Civil War

For America's freed slaves, as the Reconstruction era came to a close, so too did the promise of liberty, equality and peace. In the agricultural South, freed families were promised 40 acres and a mule — yet President Andrew Jackson returned the land to its previous Confederate owners.

With no opportunities, many were forced to become sharecroppers — where landlords allowed them to work on their land in return for a portion of the harvest, trapping them in cycles of debt. Desperate, scores of Black people migrated north to Chicago, Detroit, New York and Los Angeles — only to encounter racism and hostility there too.

Although the 13th, 14th and 15th Amendments had abolished slavery and guaranteed citizenship and voting rights, the Supreme Court rapidly undermined this progress in 1883, ruling that states could not be prohibited from discriminating against African Americans.

After abolition, many Black families were forced into sharecropping — a system designed to keep them in permanent debt and subjugation.

"Jim Crow laws formed the pillars of a socioeconomic system designed to keep Black people subjugated in poverty."

Jim Crow

Jim Crow laws formalised a policy of total segregation — separating Black and White people in all manners of life, from cradle to grave. Black babies had to be born in Black hospitals, Black corpses buried in Black graveyards. Signs reading "Whites Only" and "Colored" hung over bus stations, water fountains, toilets and building entrances.

Resistance

The NAACP, founded in 1909, became the USA's oldest and largest civil rights organisation. Its first president, Moorfield Storey, argued before the Supreme Court that Louisville had violated the 14th Amendment. Meanwhile, the Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s gave rise to a cultural explosion of Black art, literature, music and intellectual life in New York City. W.E.B. Du Bois called it a "spiritual coming of age" — transforming social disillusionment into collective pride.

  • Emmett Till (1955) — 14-year-old tortured and murdered in Mississippi. All-White jury acquitted killers. The open casket, insisted upon by his mother, galvanised the movement.
  • Rosa Parks (Dec 1955) — Arrest sparked a 381-day bus boycott led by Dr. MLK, ending with buses being desegregated.
  • March on Washington (1963) — 260,000 gathered. MLK delivered "I Have a Dream." Photos of children attacked by police dogs shocked the world.
  • Civil Rights Act 1964 — Banned discrimination by race, colour, religion, sex or national origin — after an 83-day Senate filibuster.
  • Voting Rights Act 1965 — Banned literacy tests. Black voter registration rose from 35% to 65%.
  • Selma to Montgomery (1965) — 500 marchers attacked by state troopers on national TV. The march grew to 25,000.
"Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime … shall exist within the United States."— 13th Amendment to the US Constitution, 1865
02 Chapter Two

The Land of
the Free…?

The 13th Amendment abolished slavery — but included a loophole that allowed the penal system to repress minority communities ever since. The words "except as a punishment for crime" changed everything.

Convicts leased to harvest timber in Florida, 1915 — providing cheap labour to agricultural business in the South after abolition.
The Loophole

The abolition of slavery crippled the Southern economy. Southern lawmakers immediately criminalised the emancipated Black population — arresting people for petty crimes like vagrancy and loitering, and once detained, putting them straight back to work. Convicts were leased to agricultural businesses in the South. Slavery, continued under another name.

DW Griffith's 1915 film Birth of a Nation demonised Black men, casting them as a threat to white purity. The film was almost solely responsible for the rebirth of the Ku Klux Klan — whose membership soon surged to three million. Crucially, the film is still in use by sections of the white supremacy movement today.

The War on Drugs

In 1969, President Nixon proclaimed drugs "Public Enemy No. 1" — using coded "dog whistle" language to garner support without naming Black communities directly. His cabinet adviser later confirmed the war on drugs was a deliberate attack on Black and left-wing communities. The results were devastating.

Prison Growth

The prison population exploded following the War on Drugs and Clinton's 1994 Crime Bill, which introduced mandatory life sentences after two prior convictions — including drug crimes.

Private companies build and run prisons — they need full prisons to protect their investment. Through organisations like ALEC (American Legislative Exchange Council), corporations including Walmart and Coca-Cola have been able to directly shape legislation — voting on bills alongside politicians before they go to Congress.

CCA (now CoreCivic) earned $11 million per month from illegal immigrants detained in Arizona alone. Companies including Boeing, Microsoft, JC Penney and Victoria's Secret have used prison labour — manufacturing products for pennies. Convicted felons also often lose the right to vote permanently — a direct echo of post-slavery disenfranchisement.

03 Chapter Three

Legacy &
Lasting Impact

400+ years of trafficking left consequences in every corner of the world — in healthcare, policing, wealth, governance and culture. These are not historical footnotes. They are present realities.

Systemic Racism — Health & Work
  • Black Americans were 3.57× more likely to die from Covid-19 than White Americans
  • Many White medical students still hold false beliefs about Black pain tolerance — identical to 19th-century justifications for enslaved people's mistreatment
  • UK: over ⅓ of BME workers unfairly turned down for jobs; 15% singled out for redundancy due to racism (TUC 2021)
  • African & Bangladeshi households hold 10× less wealth than White households in the UK
  • 55% of Black & mixed-race Covid patients in Brazil died vs 38% of White patients
Policing — Origins & Today
  • 1661: Barbados passed the first "slave law" — the direct precursor to modern policing
  • London Metropolitan Police (1829) inspired by Patrick Colquhoun — who owned shares in Jamaican sugar plantations
  • US first police forces created to control enslaved people, not protect all citizens
  • Black Americans are ~3× more likely to be killed by police than White Americans
  • UK: Black people 8× more likely to be stopped & searched; 43× more likely under Section 60
Reparations & Money
  • UK borrowed £20M in 1833 to compensate 46,000 slave owners — not paid off until 2015. Enslaved people received nothing.
  • France extorted reparations from Haiti for 100+ years — for "loss of property" when the enslaved people revolted
  • UCL research: 10–20% of Britain's wealthiest have significant links to slavery
  • The Black-White wealth gap in the US traces directly to centuries of systematic wealth extraction
Africa & Modern Slavery
  • 20 million guns imported to Africa in the second half of the 18th century — many made in Birmingham, UK
  • Trafficking destroyed governance systems based on kinship, consent and trust
  • 40.3 million people in modern slavery today — 24.9M in forced labour, 15.4M in forced marriage
  • Women and girls represent 71% of modern slavery victims
  • Most prevalent in Africa, then Asia and the Pacific — a largely invisible global crisis

"The post-traumatic slave syndrome — a condition that exists when a population has experienced multigenerational trauma — continues to experience oppression and institutionalised racism today."

— Dr. Joy DeGruy, 2005
04 Chapter Four

The Fight
Continues

From Black Lives Matter to reparations, statue removal to language reform — the struggle for justice, recognition and equality is very much alive today. And it is global.

Black Lives Matter

Founded in 2013 by Alicia Garza, Patrisse Cullors and Opal Tometi — after the acquittal of George Zimmerman for the murder of Trayvon Martin — Black Lives Matter has grown into one of the most significant movements of our time.

After George Floyd's murder at the hands of Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin in 2020, between 15 and 26 million protesters took to the streets in the United States alone — one of the largest movements in American history. The movement spread globally: Britain, Brazil, Colombia, Denmark, France, Canada and beyond.

In 2021, a UN Human Rights Council resolution called to investigate the root causes of systemic racism and police violence worldwide. BLM has since developed into a decentralised political and social movement fighting the legacies of slavery, police brutality and racism across every continent.

Protests outside the White House following the murder of George Floyd, May 2020. An estimated 15–26 million people joined protests across the US.
  • Over 170 Confederate monuments removed in the US between June 2020 and June 2021
  • Germany (2021) — announced the return of hundreds of artefacts to Benin, West Africa
  • Church of England — publicly apologised for inheriting three Caribbean sugar estates run with forced labour
  • Multiple UK banks and institutions issued public apologies for slave trade ties since 2020
  • Critics: "An apology as a public spectacle is not enough. It is not a PR exercise." — Professor Sir Hilary Beckles, Vice-Chancellor of the University of the West Indies

The goal is not to abolish police entirely, but to redirect funding toward mental health services, homelessness support and rehabilitation. A Washington Post analysis found that 25% of people shot by police in a six-month period were experiencing a mental health crisis — situations where armed officers may not be the most appropriate responders.

  • Sweden (since 2015) — mental health professionals deployed instead of police for relevant calls
  • 2020: Over a dozen US cities cut police budgets by more than $1.4 billion combined

Language shapes how we understand and discuss this history. Academics and anti-racism campaigners have called for a shift in how we speak about the 400-plus years of transatlantic trafficking. How we name things reflects what we value.
